Explanation:

In this case, the code-switching points appear at nouns (causality, input), verbs (output, depend on) and an adverb (at any time). The rule-based approach "noun" is accurate, but cannot find out all CSPs. The proposed method with POS tagging can find out more CSPs.

Part 2: Code-switching generation examples


Good examples generated by our proposed model
Origin: 這個 可以 這樣 分配 因為 它 有 交換性 嘛 可以 交換 嘛
　　　 (It can be this distribution because it has exchangeability to exchange)
Generation : 這個 可以 這樣 distribution 因為 它 有 exchangeability 嘛 可以 交換 嘛
Origin: 可能 每個人 都是 這樣 吧
　　　 (Everyone is probably so)
Generation : Probably 每個人 都是 這樣 吧
Origin: 哇 他 幫 你們 很多 啊
　　　 (Wow he helped you much)
Generation : Wow 他 幫 你們 很多 啊


Bad examples generated by our proposed model
Origin: 那 那 這個 其實 也就是我 剛才 畫 在這邊 的
　　　 (Well... This is actually what I have drew over here)
Generation : That that 這個 其實 that is 我 剛才 畫 在這邊 的
Origin: 有 看到 嗎 這個 應該 容易 看得出來 囉
　　　 (See? It may be seen easily)
Generation : Have 看到 嗎 這個 應該 容易 看得出來 囉
Origin:所以 我們 人 的 耳朵 跟 眼睛
　　　 (So our/people's ears and eyes)
Generation : So we people 的 耳朵 跟 眼睛
Explanation:

``causality,'' ``probably'' and ``wow'' are switched by our model in the examples. It shows that our proposed method can transform some Chinese sentences into reasonable code-switching sentences. However, it also generated terrible sentences because bad translation directly from Chinese to English. For example, ``那~那'' is a filled pause which should be translated into ``well'' in this sentence. But it cannot be correctly translated due to the constraint of our translator. Besides, ``we" and ``people " may appear simultaneously in Chinese but not in English. Hence, our model generated a strange sentence because of speaking style in different language.
